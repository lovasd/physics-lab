Simple Pendulum Experiment

Investigating the relationship between period and length of a simple pendulum

Experiment Overview

The simple pendulum experiment is a fundamental investigation in classical mechanics that explores the relationship between the period of oscillation and the length of a pendulum. This experiment demonstrates the principles of harmonic motion and provides hands-on experience with data collection and analysis.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the relationship between pendulum length and period
  • Verify the theoretical formula T = 2π√(L/g)
  • Practice precise measurement techniques
  • Analyze experimental data and calculate uncertainties
  • Determine the local gravitational acceleration

Key Concepts

Simple Harmonic Motion

Periodic motion where the restoring force is proportional to displacement

Period

Time for one complete oscillation cycle

Gravitational Acceleration

Acceleration due to gravity (g ≈ 9.81 m/s²)

Theoretical Background

The Simple Pendulum Equation

For a simple pendulum with small amplitude oscillations (θ < 15°), the period T is given by:

T = 2π√(L/g)

where:

  • T = Period of oscillation (seconds)
  • L = Length of pendulum (meters)
  • g = Gravitational acceleration (m/s²)
  • π = Pi (≈ 3.14159)

Key Assumptions

  • Small amplitude oscillations (θ < 15°)
  • Point mass bob (negligible size)
  • Massless, inextensible string
  • No air resistance or friction
  • Constant gravitational field

Expected Results

According to the theory, the period should be:

  • Independent of the mass of the bob
  • Independent of the amplitude (for small angles)
  • Proportional to the square root of the length
  • Inversely proportional to the square root of g

Required Equipment

Pendulum Setup

  • String or thread (1-2 meters)
  • Mass bob (50-200g)
  • Support stand with clamp
  • Protractor for angle measurement

Measurement Tools

  • Meter stick or tape measure
  • Digital stopwatch
  • Vernier calipers (optional)
  • Digital scale (optional)

Recording Materials

  • Data table template
  • Graph paper or computer
  • Calculator
  • Lab notebook

Experimental Procedure

1

Setup the Pendulum

Attach the string to the support stand and suspend the mass bob. Ensure the setup is stable and the string is taut.

Tips:
  • Use a secure knot or clamp for the string
  • Ensure the bob hangs freely without touching anything
  • Keep the setup away from air currents
2

Measure the Length

Measure the length L from the support point to the center of the bob. Record this measurement.

Tips:
  • Measure from the pivot point to the center of mass
  • Use a meter stick for precise measurements
  • Record length in meters with appropriate precision
3

Set Initial Amplitude

Displace the pendulum to a small angle (θ < 15°) and release it. Allow it to oscillate freely.

Tips:
  • Use a protractor to measure the angle
  • Keep amplitude small for accurate results
  • Release gently to avoid initial velocity
4

Measure the Period

Use a stopwatch to measure the time for 10 complete oscillations. Divide by 10 to get the period.

Tips:
  • Start timing after the first oscillation
  • Count complete back-and-forth cycles
  • Repeat measurements for better accuracy
5

Vary the Length

Repeat the experiment with different pendulum lengths. Collect data for at least 5 different lengths.

Tips:
  • Use lengths ranging from 0.2m to 1.0m
  • Keep the same bob and amplitude
  • Record all measurements carefully

Analysis Methods

Method 1: Direct Calculation

Use the formula g = 4π²L/T² to calculate g for each data point, then take the average.

Method 2: Linear Regression

Plot T² vs L. The slope should be 4π²/g, so g = 4π²/slope. This method is more accurate.

Uncertainty Analysis

Calculate uncertainties in length and time measurements, then propagate to find uncertainty in g.

Discussion Points

  • Sources of Error: Air resistance, string stretch, timing errors, amplitude effects
  • Improvements: Use longer strings, smaller amplitudes, multiple trials
  • Extensions: Investigate amplitude dependence, mass dependence, compound pendulums
  • Applications: Clocks, seismometers, gravity measurements
